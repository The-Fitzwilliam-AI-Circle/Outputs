# The inconsistency principle

- The same vaccine works in one population and fails in another. The inconsistency is the immunology
- Your Western blot shows a band in three of five replicates. The inconsistency is biological, not technical. Probably
- The same cytokine is pro-inflammatory in one context and anti-inflammatory in another. That's not a contradiction — it's context-dependence
- The immune system is inconsistent by design. Redundancy and degeneracy are the architecture

# The tape is now the music

- The patient sample is not a proxy for the disease. It is the disease, at that moment, in that person
- Your FACS data is not a representation of the immune system. It is the measurement. Treat it as the object of study
- The cell line is not a model of the T cell. It's a cell line. Study it as what it is
- The mouse experiment didn't model the human disease. It modeled the mouse disease. That's also interesting

# Think of the radio

- Your findings need to reach the clinician who will change practice. Write for them
- The patient wants to understand their immune system. Can you explain your work to them?
- The public health official needs a yes or no. Your nuanced mechanistic answer needs a summary
- The vaccine developer is listening. Does your basic science tell them anything actionable?

# Trust in the you of now

- Your understanding of this system is deeper than it was three years ago. Redesign the experiment accordingly
- You've seen enough histology to trust your pattern recognition. The scoring system confirms, it doesn't replace, your eye
- You know what a healthy culture looks like. Trust that knowledge
- Your hypothesis has evolved with your experience. The new version is better than the old one. Follow it

# Turn it upside down

- Instead of studying how the immune system fights infection, study how infection shapes the immune system
- What if the immune system's primary job is tissue maintenance, and defense is secondary?
- Instead of asking why autoimmunity happens, ask why it doesn't happen more often
- Flip the experiment: instead of measuring the host response to the pathogen, measure the pathogen's response to the host

# Twist the spine

- You study systemic immunity. What if this is fundamentally a local, tissue-level phenomenon?
- Move the same question from the spleen to the skin. The answer changes entirely
- You've been thinking about this as a cell-autonomous process. What if it's a population-level decision?
- Shift from protein to metabolism. The cell's metabolic state is the immunological state

# Use an old idea

- Gruber and Durham described desensitization in 1903. The principle still works and still isn't fully understood
- Passive antibody transfer. Behring won the first Nobel Prize for it. It's still the fastest way to protect a patient
- The side-chain theory of antibody formation was wrong in detail but right in principle. What else was right in principle?
- Cross-reactivity was considered a nuisance. Heterologous immunity suggests it's a strategy

# Use an unacceptable color

- Study the immune response to the commensal, not the pathogen. The tolerance is the interesting part
- Use the inbred mouse strain everyone says is "wrong" for this disease model. What's different?
- Measure the immune response in the elderly, not in healthy young adults. That's where the system is stressed
- Study the organ that isn't considered immunological. Adipose tissue, bone, brain — they all have immune systems

# Use fewer notes

- One knockout, one readout, one interpretation. Simplify
- Measure one cytokine. The right one tells you more than a 30-plex panel
- One mouse strain, one pathogen, one time point. Master that before you expand
- The 4-color flow panel that answers the question is better than the 20-color panel that confuses it

# Use filters

- Gate on the live cells only. Then gate on the activated cells only. Then gate on the antigen-specific cells only. Now you're looking at the response
- Filter your patient cohort to the most extreme responders and non-responders. Ignore the middle
- Only analyze the tissue-resident cells. What does the immune system look like without the circulating visitors?
- Restrict your analysis to the first 24 hours. The decision is made early. Everything after is execution

# Use "unqualified" people

- The bioinformatician sees patterns in your data that you miss because they don't know what's "supposed" to be there
- The medical student asks "why do we need the spleen?" and you realize you can't fully answer
- The engineer looks at the immune system and sees a control problem, not a recognition problem
- The patient describes their symptoms in language that reveals something the clinical terminology hides

# Water

- The immune system flows. Cells circulate, cytokines diffuse, antigens drain through lymphatics. Study the movement
- Inflammation is a flood. Resolution is the water receding. What's left on the banks?
- The mucosal surface is a waterline between self and non-self. The interface is the immune system's primary workplace
- Let the biology be fluid. The categories — Th1, Th2, Th17 — are dams you built. The cells are the water

# What are you really thinking about just now? Incorporate

- You keep thinking about the patient who didn't respond to treatment. That case is the experiment you should design
- You're distracted by the structural biology paper. The shape of that receptor is telling you something about function
- You're worried about reproducibility. That worry is a valid research direction
- The image from the microscope this morning stays in your mind. Go back and look at it more carefully

# What is the reality of the situation?

- Your mouse model doesn't get the human disease. It gets something adjacent. Know the gap
- The in vitro concentration you use is 100x what exists in tissue. Acknowledge that
- The patient population is heterogeneous. Your n=20 contains at least three different immune stories
- The reagent lot changed. Half your data is on old lot, half on new. That's the reality. Address it

# What mistakes did you make last time?

- Last time you didn't include the isotype control. This time you did. But did you also check the FMO?
- The fixation protocol killed the epitope last time. Use a different fixative
- You read out too early and missed the peak. Add the later time point
- You pooled the samples before you should have and lost the per-animal variation

# What would your closest friend do?

- Your collaborator would say: you have enough data. Stop collecting and start analyzing
- The PI down the hall would say: show me the controls first
- Your grad school mentor would say: what's the simplest experiment that tests this?
- Your closest colleague would say: you're overcomplicating this. What's the actual question?

# What wouldn't you do?

- You'd never study the immune response to a meal. But every meal is an antigen challenge
- You'd never phenotype the red blood cells. But they carry immune complexes and complement
- You'd never look at the immune system in a healthy person. But that's the baseline nobody has defined properly
- You'd never just watch cells for twelve hours without measuring anything. But the behavior is the data

# Work at a different speed

- Run the kinetic experiment with time points every 15 minutes instead of every 24 hours
- Slow down: follow one patient for five years instead of fifty patients for six months
- Accelerate: screen a hundred compounds at once with a rough readout before perfecting the assay for three
- The immune response moves fast. Your experimental design might be too slow to catch the early events

# You are an engineer

- The vaccine needs to reach cold-chain-limited settings. The immunology is solved. The delivery is the problem
- Design the assay for the clinic, not the research lab. Simplify
- The CAR T cell is an engineered product. Think about manufacturing, not just biology
- Immune monitoring needs to work at scale. Build the pipeline, not just the proof-of-concept

# You can only make one dot at a time

- One experiment, properly controlled, fully analyzed, before the next one
- One patient sample, handled perfectly, is worth more than ten handled hastily
- One figure at a time. Make it clear, complete, and honest. Then move on
- The comprehensive immune atlas takes years. Today, characterize one cell population well

# You don't have to be ashamed of using your own ideas

- Your hypothesis came from clinical observation, not from the literature. That's a strength
- The unpublished observation you made two years ago — it's time to build an experiment around it
- You noticed something in the data that no one else has commented on. Write it up
- Your model of how this works is different from the published one. Test it. You might be right
